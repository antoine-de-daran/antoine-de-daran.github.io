# Writing Guidelines

This file defines the voice, tone, and style for all written content on this blog. Follow these rules when writing or editing any page, post, or description.

## Voice

- **Factual over opinionated.** State what is, not what you believe. If something is an opinion, make it obviously one — or cut it.
- **Humble and altruistic.** Share what you learned, not how great you are. Write to help the reader, not to impress them.
- **Honest.** Do not claim things that are not true or stretch the truth for effect. If "most" is not accurate, do not write "most."

## Style

- Short sentences. Clear intent. One idea per sentence.
- Use simple, direct words. "like" not "love." "use" not "leverage." "help" not "empower."
- Write like you are talking to a colleague, not pitching to a crowd.
- Friendly and warm where appropriate (FAQ, contact sections). Precise and dry where appropriate (technical posts, project descriptions).

## What to avoid

- **Marketing language.** No superlatives, no hype, no calls to action disguised as prose.
- **Empty statements.** If a sentence could appear on any tech blog and still make sense, it probably says nothing. Cut it. Examples of what to cut:
  - "I believe in the power of open source and sharing knowledge with the community."
  - "Building solutions that are both practical and elegant."
  - "Good ideas spread faster when they're shared."
- **AI-generated slop.** Generic filler, vague inspiration, hollow abstractions. If it sounds like it was generated to fill space, it was — delete it.
- **Sensationalism.** No clickbait titles, no dramatic framing, no "you won't believe" energy.

## Self-review checklist

After writing, re-read every sentence with skepticism:

1. Is this true? Can I back it up?
2. Does this sentence teach the reader something or is it filler?
3. Would I say this out loud to a colleague without feeling awkward?
4. Could this line appear on any generic tech blog? If yes, rewrite or remove.
5. Is there a shorter way to say the same thing?
